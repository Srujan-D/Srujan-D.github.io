Towards a Digital Inida- The UPI Way!

UPI is an instant real-time payment system developed by National Payments Corporation of India. This technology has changed the way our nation perceives cashless transactions and has opened up many new avenues. Here, we discuss how exactly it has impacted the country and what future potential prospects does it hold.

Srujan Deolasee
Sep 20, 2019 4 min read Personal

Let us rewind five years of our life. We live in the era of Internet Banking- the supposedly ‘better’ alternative to offline and physical means of banking. But was it? Net banking requires our account number and type, bank name, and IFSC code. Most banks add a new payee after 12 hours, which means your friend will get the money long after he needs it. Not to mention the efforts you put in to type all this on your phone.

Enter UPI or United Payments Interface:

The very intention of introducing UPI was to get rid of all this hassle. In layman’s terms, UPI is nothing but an email-id for your money. Something which your bank uses to do money transfers and payments. UPI uses Immediate Payment Service (IMPS) to do this. IMPS is faster than NEFT, as its acts immediately, and also, it’s 24x7x365. Thus, UPI makes it easier to make online payments than a digital wallet or credit or debit card.

Let’s look at the architecture of UPI.

  1. You link your UPI wallet with a bank, after which you’ll get a virtual address for the same.
  2. You use this virtual address while making a payment via your bank.
  3. UPI would use the information it has to map the virtual address of the sending and receiving banks to the existing bank accounts and then initiate a credit and debit transaction.

Impact on the Country:

Due to its perks, UPI is in progress to overtake the other online wallets. Even if the latter outperforms the former on a cumulative basis, the UPI app is growing faster per month. In February this year, transactions exceeding Rs.1.01 lakh crore (that’s over $14 billion) were recorded via UPI, far above mobile wallets Rs.14,279 crore.

UPI deserves this credit as it has three significant advantages over online wallets:

  1. Simpler to use
  2. No KYC (know your customer) KYC is not required for UPI as it is just like any other internet banking transaction.
  3. Interoperability- You can’t use Paytm to send money to your friend using Google Pay. UPI overcomes this.

This has forced online wallets to make themselves more lucrative by offering many cash backs. While this has benefited them, it has also increased the bank’s activities, as ultimately, the bank is where the real money lies. Thus, the introduction of UPI has benefitted both parties, while the people are the most significant gainers.

Above all, UPI would be good for the economy as it would give way to mass cashless payments. The use of hard cash has come down, and the economy has become more transparent. Digital payments are growing quickly within the country; however, they’re still a fraction of the payments. Most of the transactions involving small amounts of money are still in cash. The ease and convenience UPI offers has helped individuals prefer to pay their maids, milkman, newspaper vendor, etc., digitally than in cash. Today, many find bank visits cumbersome. With UPI, we feel the same about visiting an ATM. There are over 40 million merchants in India, and out of those, only about a million use card readers. With UPI, they won’t even need those archaic machines. Not to forget the costs per transaction that come with the likes of VISA, MasterCard, etc. UPI costs only 50 paise per transaction. All in all, the impact UPI has had and will continue to have on our country is too large to neglect.

Looking Ahead: What UPI Can be:

There is one feature in UPI, which is ‘mandates’. Mandates or standing instructions mean that UPI can become the go-to payment option for all recurring payments like SIPs, Mutual Fund payments, monthly subscriptions etc. Also, the withdrawal of the biometric Aadhaar-based payment feature will make it unusable for those who do not own a smartphone, i.e. about 30% of India’s population is deprived of the choice to use UPI or any other digital payment solution. So, bringing these back will only boost UPI.

A Green Approach to do that:

Every year we produce more than 2 million tonnes of e-waste, and the majority comes from used mobile phones. We can build our operating system (O.S.) having the features of calling, texting, camera, and UPI based app. We will load this on our used phone. Selling these to the poverty-ridden sector at a low price will only help widen the reach of UPI while also reducing e-waste. I’ll conclude by saying that for UPI to become the leader in digital transactions, it must drive large-scale adoption of digital payments. There needs to be an extensive merchant-acceptance network, both online and offline because that is where the real push for ‘Digital India’ will come from.
